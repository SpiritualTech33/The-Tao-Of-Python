# Chapter 6 — The Mirror

> *"How do you know your code works? Because you ran it once and it seemed fine?"*

---

## The Question

A surgeon cannot practice on living patients.

A pilot cannot learn to land in a storm by crashing.

But a programmer *can* discover that their function is broken by shipping it and watching the system fail.

This happens. It is not inevitable. **Tests are the mechanism by which programmers practice without consequence.**

---

## What Testing Is, At Its Root

A test is a question asked of code.

*"If I give you these inputs — what do you give me back?"*

When the code answers correctly, the test passes. When it answers incorrectly, the test fails. The test does not judge you — it informs you. It is an honest mirror.

The untested program is not just unknown — it is unknowable. You cannot trust what you have not measured. You cannot improve what you cannot observe.

## The Unusual Kata

Chapter 6's katas are different from all previous chapters.

In Chapters 1–5, the kata file provided the tests. You provided the implementations.

Here, you will sometimes provide both. You will write the function *and* the tests that prove it works.

This is a shift. You are no longer just the builder. You are also the critic. The architect and the inspector.

That dual role is what professional software development requires.

---

## The Deeper Why

Descartes sat by a fire and decided to doubt everything he thought he knew. He stripped away assumption after assumption until he arrived at something he could not doubt: *cogito ergo sum.* I think, therefore I am.

Testing is the programmer's *cogito.* It strips away assumption. It says: *I will not believe this works until I have proved it.* Not once. Every time. On demand.

The tested codebase is a codebase that has passed through doubt and emerged on the other side.

That is a kind of knowing the untested codebase can never have.
